\subsection{Introduction}

\textit{Knowledge Graphs} (KGs) \cite{hogan_knowledge_2022, angles_survey_2008} are becoming prominent database architectures in recent years. They are often favored over more traditional, relational databases, because their ability to provide more flexibility in terms of schema and data evolution \cite{hogan_knowledge_2022, angles_survey_2008}. This characteristic is especially appealing in the current state of accelerated software developments and improving trend in integrations of data systems due to advancements in \textit{Generative AI}. Moreover, Knowledge Graphs have more intuitive data modeling language and representation by design.

Property graphs (PGs) \cite{angles_property_2018} are one of the most used forms of KGs which experience increasing interest with the popularization of Neo4j \cite{miller_graph_2013} and similar enterprise applications \cite{angles_foundations_2017}. They extend the purest form of KGs the \textit{Directed edge-labeled graphs} by allowing types, \textit{property-value} sets and labels to be associated with both nodes and edges \cite{hogan_knowledge_2022}. This feature makes them even more flexible than other forms of graph databases.

In the purest traditional KG model, in the RDF \cite{cyganiak_rdf_2014} nodes and edges cannot hold properties. Therefore, attaching metadata (such as timestamps, weights, or sources) requires structural reification, turning a relationship into a new node and generating multiple new edges \cite{hernandez_reifying_nodate}. This inevitably leads to a massive explosion in graph size and general graph bloat. PGs bypass this structural bloat because the entity we want to represent can often be turned into a native property \cite{angles_rdf_nodate}. However, because the PG solution space is much larger, clearer theoretical guidelines are required to avoid arbitrary modeling choices and maintain semantic consistency.

To establish these guidelines, we can rely on \textit{Truthmaker theory} \cite{guarino_reification_2018}. Truthmaker theory provides a framework to systematically identify what must be represented as a node (``strong truthmakers'', such as complex events or nested propositions) and what can be safely represented natively as an edge property (``weak truthmakers'', such as contingent qualities, dates, or weights). 

\subsection{Problem 1: Strong Truthmakers and Nested Propositions}

To illustrate the application of strong truthmakers, consider the statement: \textit{``The detective supposes that the butler killed the gardener.''}

\textbf{Identifying the Strong Truthmaker:} The core of this statement is a ``supposition''—a nested proposition or mental event. According to Truthmaker theory, this acts as a strong truthmaker \cite{guarino_reification_2018}. Because PGs do not allow an edge to point to another edge, the act of ``Killing'' cannot just be an edge between the Butler and the Gardener; it must be reified structurally into its own \texttt{Event} node. 

\textbf{Applying the PG Structure:} To maintain model consistency, the Detective, Butler, and Gardener are created as distinct \texttt{Person} nodes. The Butler and Gardener are connected to the \texttt{Event} node as the perpetrator and victim, respectively. The relationship between the Detective and the Event node is modeled as a simple \texttt{THINKS} edge. The specific details of that thought (e.g., that it is a ``supposition'' rather than a ``fact'') act as weak truthmakers, which can be elegantly handled by attaching them as native key-value properties directly to the \texttt{THINKS} edge.

\subsection{Problem 2: Weak Truthmakers and Graph Conciseness}

For our second scenario, consider a historical football fact: \textit{``Netherlands beat Sweden in the 2026 World Cup 5-1.''}

\textbf{Identifying the Weak Truthmaker:} This scenario demonstrates where PGs handle reification effortlessly. Assuming our domain requirements only dictate noting the football teams and their past historical results, we do not need a strong truthmaker. The result of the match (5-1), the year (2026), and the tournament name are simple contingent qualities \cite{guarino_reification_2018}. 

\textbf{The PG Solution:} The Netherlands and Sweden are modeled as distinct \texttt{FootballTeam} nodes connected by a single \texttt{BEAT} edge. The minimal weak truthmakers are then safely reified as native key-value properties directly on that edge. 

\textbf{Contrast with Strong Truthmakers \& RDF:} If our database needed to track which specific players participated in this exact match, we would be forced to use a strong truthmaker and create a structural \texttt{MatchEvent} node. However, for just the historical results, PGs allow us to rely on weak truthmakers natively on the edge. In an RDF database, achieving this exact same logical mapping would still force structural graph bloat upon us, requiring intermediary nodes just to hold the score and date \cite{hernandez_reifying_nodate}.

\subsection{Conclusion and Limitations}

Clear, well-reasoned reifications are essential in Property Graphs. Because the PG solution space is highly flexible, it is easier to implement arbitrary architectures that become difficult to query later \cite{angles_foundations_2017}. Truthmakers provide a vital philosophical framework to systematically decide when to use a structural node (for strong truthmakers) and when to use an edge property (for weak truthmakers). Applying this framework successfully dictated the structural node expansion needed for the detective's nested supposition, while preserving the concise, native edge properties for the historical football match result.

Despite their flexibility, standard PGs still face a severe limitation: a relationship (edge) cannot easily be referenced by another entity. \textbf{Meta-Property Graphs (MPG)}\cite{sadoughi_meta-property_2025} aim to solve this specific research gap. They natively allow any sub-structure of an MPG (such as an edge, a set of properties, or an entire subgraph) to be reified as a single node, making it a ``first-class citizen.'' This approach completely breaks down the data-metadata barrier, enabling modelers to assign properties or labels to a node that explicitly reifies a specific sub-structure without needing clunky structural workarounds.
